What Killers Write

06.28.2022



More and more frequently it seems, mass shootings are accompanied by some kind of "manifesto." This is hardly a new phenomenon—Ted Kaczynski's and Timothy McVeigh's far-right manifestos still loom large in the collective imagination (alongside the carnage these documents were meant to justify, of course.)

If you've ever taken the time to read one of these screeds—be it Kaczynski's "Industrial Society and its Future" or the more recent examples penned by the Christchurch, El Paso, and Buffalo shooters—any air of gravitas around these documents quickly melts away. Not only do they tend to be poorly written and reasoned, but the content is hardly groundbreaking. Kaczynski and Tarrant (Christchurch) both regurgitate far-right arguments and talking points that most audiences have encountered countless times before. In the age of the internet, plenty of people write strikingly similar things and have no problem disseminating them without killing anyone.

If you're like me, your morbid curiosity about documents like these surpasses how they are typically handled by news sites: an acknowledgement of their existence and a one-or-two sentence summary of the content. Certainly, there is plenty more to learn here. Many people more qualified than me have analyzed collections of killers' writing to, for example, create psychological profiles. There's also plenty of research on the function manifestos like these serve in extremist internet communities in coordinating and mythologizing attacks.

What I want to show here, instead, is a concise birds-eye view of how these documents relate to one another, and some macro trends. After scouring the internet for over 50 different pieces of writing from high-profile mass killers, I put together what I think is an informative way to organize and categorize these documents (and thus, their writers.)

This "Columbine"/"Christchurch" two-type classification of mass killers seems apt, but it's worth emphasizing that the relationship between these two classifications is not completely clean-cut. As the network graph shows, these two traditions have evolved together—you can follow the chain of references from Buffalo back to Columbine.

Some noteworthy exceptions:
There is also not such a clean divide in the content of these documents as the visualizations may suggest. For example, there is plenty of racism to be found in the nihilistic/grievance group: Eric Harris (Columbine) made many deferential references to Nazis throughout his journal, and even liked to pepper in German phrases. Harris wrote despicable things about gays, Jewish people, Black people, and women. But in contrast with many of the other hard-right killers, he seemed to revere the Nazis primarily for their capacity for mass cruelty itself, rather than any specific political ideas.

If you recall your history the Nazis came up with a "final solution" to the Jewish problem. Kill them all. Well, in case you haven't figured it out yet, I say "KILL MANKIND." No one should survive.
Eric Harris


Contrast this with the way the Great Replacement-motivated killers self-conceptualize as part of a grand historical struggle; their actions, they believe, will seem rational to those who are willing to accept the "truth" about race relations.

Many other killers seemed to connect with Harris's particular brand of nihilism—no other specific figure in the collection of texts was referenced by name more frequently. Randy Stair and Alvaro Castillo, in particular, write about Harris like a hero and cite him as an inspiration for what they eventually did.

There are other exceptions in the other group (characterized above as racist rationalizers) as well. For example, Christopher Dorner, who killed 5 and injured 6 in a series of shootings in California targeting law enforcement, wrote impersonally about how he was motivated to kill police officers in order to correct problems he saw in how the LAPD interacted with the community, particularly along racial lines. Still racially motivated, but hardly white supremacist.

I am here to change and make policy. The culture of LAPD versus the community and honest/good officers needs to and will change. I am here to correct and calibrate your morale compasses to true north.
Christopher Dorner


There's also James Holmes, who killed 12 and injured 70 in the 2012 Aurora, Colorado shooting. Holmes' writing is categorized in the rationalizer group, but primarily due to style rather than content. He wrote dispassionately, but about himself—adopting the tone of a professional taking notes on a subject.

Self Diagnosis of a Broken Mind Dysphoric Mania. Generalized anxiety disorder/ social anxiety disorder /OCD / PTSD (chronic)/ Asperger syndrome / autism. ADHD. Schizophrenia. Body dysmorphic disorder. Borderline, narcissistic, anxious, avoidant and obsessive compulsive personality disorder. Chronic insomnia. Psychosis. Trichotillomania. Adjustment disorder. Pain disorder. Restless leg syndrome.
James Holmes


While Holmes' self-studying tone is unique, many other killers make specific references to mental health disorders—some self-diagnosed, but others not. And a recurring topic in several texts is the ineffectiveness of the treatment (often medication) they were receiving. That's perhaps a direction to explore more in the future.

Some other things:
As a side note, in the aftermath of Columbine, Marilyn Manson's music was often speculated to be a potential inspiration of the attack, as Harris and Klebold's journals contain many references to Manson's music. Out of curiosity, I took the lyrics from Manson's 1996 album Antichrist Superstar to see where they'd land. To be sure, they fell firmly in the nihilist cluster, but not particularly close to Harris and/or Klebold—there is no exceptional similarity.

One more thing to note with respect to the network graph is the extent to which certain killers valorized others. In particular, Adam Lanza (Sandy Hook) was noteworthy in the sheer volume of references he made to other killers. He references nine other killers included in this analysis, and many, many others who were not. Lanza's obsessions with other killers is well documented: He created a massive spreadsheet containing minute details on five hundred killers. He also edited Wikipedia entries on various killers, typically adding details regarding the firearms they used.

There's a widely shared article from npr that argues that we shouldn't use the word "manifesto" to describe documents like these since doing so "may elevate" the racist content, "in the eyes of those who might want to copy this person's actions, to something more than it might really have been." I tried to be mindful of this when using this word by couching it in quotes, but chose not to avoid it completely—I think doing so would lead to confusion since this is the word most people are familiar with. Similarly, others have pointed out that even using killers' names may "gives many of them what they want, encourages future fame-seeking offenders, and appears to increase the likelihood of contagion and copycat effects." But I felt that this too was unavoidable without causing confusion.

More detail on the clustering methodology:
Looking at these types of charts, people often ask what the x and y axes represent. It's a bit unintuitive since they don't represent anything specific about the content by themselves. The original embeddings, generated with doc2vec, had a vector size of 20. To scale this down to just 2 dimensions for presentation, I used an algorithm called UMAP ( Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection). For more information on how either of algorithms work, see here for documentation on doc2vec or here for UMAP.
